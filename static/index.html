Global Vulnerabilities to Urban Transmission of Yellow Fever Virus

In 2016, 11 foreign workers from Angola carrying the yellow fever (YF) virus travelled to urban areas in China. This led to the first reported case of YF ever in Asia. More alarmingly, transmission occurred despite China having a policy that requires all international travellers arriving from YF-endemic countries, including Angola, to provide proof of vaccination. The confluence of climate change, rapid urbanization and international air travel are accelerating the globalization of mosquito-borne viruses such as dengue, chikungunya, Zika and yellow fever.

Where are Travellers Going?

8 million of these travellers went to YF-endemic cities or surrounding areas, while the other 12 million travelled to YF-suitable cities or nearby regions. This shows that the risk of transmission not only extends to other endemic areas, but also to suitable populations.

Proof of Vaccination

Around 7 million went to YF-suitable cities in countries that do not require proof of vaccination upon arrival. Similarly, 0.1 million travellers went to endemic cities in countries where such proof is not required.

Conclusion

Several problems exist to further worsen the potential for a YF outbreak, including:

1. The limited existing global stock of the YF vaccine
2. The inconsistent implementation of vaccination policies even across countries that have them
3. Increased ease of global transport

Several of the cities suitable for YF transmission also house some of the largest populations and receive the highest traveller volumes from YF-endemic areas. Since less than 40% of travellers from YF-endemic to suitable cities are required to provide proof of vaccination upon arrival, this poses a greater risk to the health of citizens in these cities.

Similarly, less than 25% of travellers from non-endemic to endemic cities are required to provide proof of vaccination upon arrival. Not only are these individuals in danger of becoming infected, but also of potentially exporting the virus to their home countries.
